# Rinascimento — Game Design Document
*Seed prompt for coding agent / implementation reference*

---

## Overview

**Rinascimento** is a cozy city builder set in Renaissance Italy (1400s–1500s). The player acts as a patron-ruler whose goal is to create a city where art and culture can flourish. It is not a survival game, not a logistics puzzle, and not a pathfinding optimizer. The core loop is about **managing relationships, priorities, and resources** to commission and produce great works of art.

The closest visual reference is **Dorfromantik** — low-poly, isometric 3D, warm and inviting. The UI reference is the attached mockup: faction panel on the left, active commissions on the right, resource bar across the top, building palette across the bottom, and the city occupying the center.

## Core Resources

**Design principle:** A resource earns a place in the top bar only if the player makes interesting decisions about it. Numbers the player never actively chooses about live in side panels as *status*, not as headline resources. There are exactly three headline resources plus one status pool.

### Headline Resources (top bar)

Each does a distinct job — none overlap, and only Inspiration needs active attention:

| Resource | Role | Description | Example decision it creates |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Florins (f)** | What you **spend** | Currency, generated by economic buildings (markets, banking houses). Active, moment-to-moment. | "Second marble supplier, or upgrade the piazza?" |
| **Inspiration** | What you **cultivate** | The signature resource. Fuels artist productivity. Generated primarily by the Piazza, scales with population in range and displayed Masterworks. Spatial — tied to the Piazza radius, so managing it overlaps with how you build rather than being a separate chore. | "My ateliers are starved — expand the piazza radius, build a garden, or display a finished masterwork?" |
| **Prestige** | What you **earn** | Cultural reputation and core progress toward the Renaissance. Less a currency you spend, more a score that gates things — higher Prestige attracts better artists and unlocks higher faction tiers. Accumulates as you succeed; not babysat. | (Not spent — earned. The satisfying number that goes up.) |

### Status Pool (visible, but not a top-bar resource)

| Status | Description |
|---|---|
| **Population / Workers** | The city's labor pool. Not spent like currency, but the two-pass worker allocation makes it matter. Shown in the top bar alongside resources for visibility. |

### Deliberately NOT resources

- **Patronage was cut.** It overlapped almost entirely with the three faction relationship bars — tracking both a Patronage currency *and* three faction meters was redundant and tedious. The faction relationships now *are* your patronage.
- **Materials are not a stockpile.** Pigment, marble, etc. are never a flowing inventory the player manages. They appear only as a binary capacity status on supplier buildings (e.g., "Pigment Trader 2/3 painters"). See Material Suppliers section.
- **Faction relationships** (×3) live in the left side panel, not the resource bar. See Factions section.

### Summary

- **Top bar:** Florins, Inspiration, Prestige, Population
- **Left side panel:** Three faction relationship bars (Church, Nobles, Guilds)
- **On buildings:** Material capacity status

This keeps the game lean — never a spreadsheet — while ensuring every visible number creates a real decision or marks real progress.

## City Building — Neighborhood System

**Key principle: players do not place individual houses.** Instead:

- Players designate **neighborhood zones** (e.g., "Workers' Quarter", "Artisan District") and they fill in organically
- Major landmarks (Cathedral, Palazzo, Piazza, Atelier) are placed individually and deliberately
- The city grows around these anchors
- No micromanagement of individual citizen paths or house-by-house placement

### Housing Tiers
Neighborhoods upgrade as the city grows and prosperity increases:
1. Cottage
2. Townhouse
3. Villa
4. Palazzo
5. Grand Palazzo

Major patron families become associated with specific named Palazzos as they rise in prominence.

---

## Road System

Roads are **fully automatic** — players never place road tiles.

- Nearby buildings auto-form **neighborhood clusters** with internal road connections
- Each cluster automatically connects outward to the nearest **hub building**
- Hub buildings: Piazza, Cathedral, Market, Guildhall
- Roads are grid-aligned (north/south/east/west only — no diagonals)
- Road connections recalculate on building placement/removal (dirty-flag system)
- Visual result: organic street network that looks like a Renaissance city without player effort

---

## Population & Workers

- Population is the city's **workforce**
- Housing capacity determines maximum population
- Population grows automatically when housing exists and service buildings are present
- Service buildings (Bakery, Market, Tavern) do not require granular supply chains — they simply **unlock population growth past thresholds** (abstracted)
- Buildings require workers to function; unstaffed buildings are inactive

### Worker Allocation — Two-Pass Algorithm
1. **First pass:** Fill all buildings to their **minimum staffing** level, in priority order (essential > production > luxury; proximity to housing as tiebreaker)
2. **Second pass:** Distribute remaining workers up to **maximum capacity** for efficiency bonuses

Buildings below minimum: inactive (desaturated, no animations, tooltip explains why)
Buildings at minimum: active at base efficiency
Buildings above minimum: active with efficiency bonus

## Factions

Three major factions compete for the player's attention and resources:

### Church
- Wants: Frescoes, altarpieces, religious sculptures, cathedrals
- Rewards: High florins, moderate prestige
- Consequence if neglected: Restricts city operations, reduces inspiration

### Noble Families
- Subdivided into named rival families (e.g., Medici, Strozzi, Pazzi)
- Each family wants portraits, secular art, grand palazzos
- Rewards: High prestige, special commissions, artist access
- Families develop rivalries and bid against each other for the same artists
- Consequence if neglected: Withdraw patronage, poach your artists

### Guilds
- Represent craftsmen, merchants, and artisans
- Want: Public art, market murals, civic buildings
- Rewards: Economic bonuses, worker efficiency
- Consequence if neglected: Slower population growth, reduced production

### Faction Relationship Bars
Each faction has a visible relationship meter (Hostile → Cautious → Neutral → Friendly → Devoted). Displayed in the left panel at all times.

## Artists & the Atelier System

### Artist Types
- Painters
- Sculptors
- Architects
- Illuminators (manuscript art)

### Master-Apprentice System
- Artists begin as Apprentices and can grow into Masters
- Masters can take on Apprentices, teaching them and accelerating their growth
- This creates **compound cultural progression** over time
- A Master's death or departure is a significant setback

### Artist Needs
- Access to their material supplier (Pigment Trader for painters, Marble Supplier for sculptors, etc.)
- Sufficient Inspiration (drawn from the Piazza radius)
- A functioning Atelier building with available workers

### Artist Personality (light system)
- Artists have preferences and traits (e.g., "refuses secular commissions", "rivals with another artist", "requires ultramarine pigment specifically")
- These create interesting constraints and stories without being overwhelming

## Material Suppliers

Material suppliers have **limited capacity** — this is the primary scarcity mechanic:

| Supplier | Serves |
|---|---|
| Pigment Trader | Painters (capacity: e.g., 3 painters) |
| Marble Supplier | Sculptors |
| Goldsmith | Illuminators, luxury commissions |
| Timber Yard | Construction, woodworkers |
| Paper Mill | Scholars, illuminators |

When a supplier is at capacity, additional artists of that type cannot work. Players must build additional suppliers or upgrade existing ones. This forces meaningful prioritization: which artists get materials?

---

## Commissions

Commissions are the primary goal-driving mechanic. They are offered by factions and named patron families.

Each commission has:
- A **requesting faction or family**
- A **required artist type** (painter, sculptor, etc.)
- A **required material** (pigments, marble, etc.)
- A **duration** in months
- A **reward** (florins, prestige, and/or a faction relationship boost)
- A **deadline** (optional — creates gentle tension)

Multiple commissions can be active simultaneously. The right panel displays current active commissions with progress bars.

Completing commissions:
- Produces a named **Masterwork** (e.g., "Portrait of Lorenzo", "Cathedral Fresco")
- Masterworks displayed in the Piazza or gallery buildings boost Inspiration permanently
- Each Masterwork contributes to the player's overall Prestige score

---

## Inspiration System

Inspiration is the lifeblood of artistic productivity:

- The **Piazza** is the primary Inspiration generator
- Inspiration scales with: population in range, displayed Masterworks, quality of surrounding buildings
- The Piazza has a visible **radius** — buildings within it benefit; buildings outside do not
- Upgrading the Piazza extends radius and boosts output
- Secondary sources: Gardens, Fountains, Libraries, completed civic buildings

Artists outside the Piazza's radius produce art more slowly or not at all.

## Building Categories

**Building design test:** Every building must either *unlock* something (a commission type, a population tier, an artist technique) or *passively boost* something (inspiration, florins, prestige). No building requires active management of its internal operations. You build it; it does its job.

### Civic / Landmark (player-placed individually, function as road hubs)
- **Piazza** — mandatory hub, generates Inspiration, displays Masterworks
- **Cathedral** — Church hub, unlocks religious commissions
- **Market** — Guild hub, generates Florins
- **Guildhall** — Guild hub, unlocks craft commissions
- **Palazzo** — Noble family hub, unlocks patron commissions
- **Banking House** — enables larger noble-family commissions (Medici flavor), boosts florin generation

### Production / Artistic
- **Atelier / Bottega** — where painters and sculptors work; requires workers and material access
- **Scriptorium** — for illuminators
- **Sculpture Workshop** — for sculptors

### Suppliers (capacity-limited — the core scarcity mechanic)
- Pigment Trader, Marble Supplier, Goldsmith, Timber Yard, Paper Mill, Glassblower (unlocks stained-glass commissions)

### Housing / Neighborhoods
- Player zones neighborhoods; these auto-fill with housing tier appropriate to city prosperity

### Service (unlock population-growth thresholds — built once, then passive)
- Bakery, Market Stall, Tavern, Bathhouse, Apothecary (unlocks plague-prevention passive), Public Well

### Social & Cultural Life (make the city feel inhabited; light inspiration/narrative texture)
- **Tavern** — artists gather here; small inspiration boost; triggers rivalry/gossip events between artists
- **Library / Studiolo** — boosts inspiration for nearby ateliers; unlocks classical-technique research
- **School** — speeds up apprentice development (humanist education)
- **Anatomical Theatre** — unlocks artist technique improvements (Renaissance artists studied anatomy)

### Religious & Ceremonial (depth for the Church faction beyond the cathedral)
- **Monastery** — produces illuminated manuscripts; quiet inspiration source
- **Chapel** — neighborhood-level religious building; keeps Church faction happy without a second cathedral
- **Baptistery** — pure prestige; unlocks higher-tier Church commissions

### Trade & Economy (unlock commissions and patrons — never supply chains)
- **Wool Merchant** — unlocks tapestry commissions (a real Renaissance art form)
- **Spice Trader** — prestige building; attracts wealthy patrons; boosts florin generation

### Decorative / Inspiration Boosters (purely beautify + boost inspiration)
- Fountain, Garden, Colonnade, Loggia, Sculpture Display, Gallery Wall
- **Memorial Column / Obelisk** — pure prestige display; marks completed campaigns or Masterworks

---

## Building Diversity Incentives

- **Diminishing returns:** Multiple identical buildings of the same type yield less per additional building
- **Complementary bonuses:** Certain building combinations grant bonuses (e.g., Tavern near Atelier boosts artist happiness; Garden near Piazza boosts Inspiration output; Library near Atelier speeds artist progress)
- These incentives make diverse cities emerge organically without arbitrary restrictions

---

## Inactive Building Feedback

When a building cannot function:
- It desaturates visually
- Activity animations stop (no chimney smoke, no movement)
- Hover tooltip explains the specific reason: "Needs 2 more workers", "Pigment Trader at capacity", "Outside Piazza inspiration radius"

## The Goal: Achieving the Renaissance

The goal of a playthrough is to **catalyze a Renaissance** in your city. You are not accumulating a score — you are creating the *conditions* for a cultural movement to ignite. The Renaissance is a threshold the city crosses: a moment, not a number.

### Why a confluence, not a single meter

Historically the Renaissance emerged from several things happening at once — wealth, humanism, artistic innovation, and cross-pollination between disciplines. The victory condition mirrors this: it requires **multiple conditions to be true simultaneously**, so the player must develop a *well-rounded* flourishing rather than min-maxing one stat. Renaissance is not one genius's achievement; it is a city that created the conditions for multiple geniuses across multiple fields to flourish at the same time.

### Trigger conditions (all must hold together)

- A target number of **Masterworks** completed across **multiple art forms** (not just paintings — sculpture, architecture, manuscripts)
- **All three factions** at Friendly or above (Church, Nobles, and Guilds all invested in the culture)
- A **Master-level artist** in at least two or three disciplines
- **Inspiration** sustained above a threshold (the city genuinely feels alive)
- At least one **landmark commission** completed — an era-defining work (a great cathedral fresco, a monumental sculpture, a civic monument)

### Game arc this produces

- **Early game:** survive economically, attract your first artists, establish the piazza
- **Mid game:** factions pull you in different directions; you make tradeoffs about which art forms to develop; master-apprentice chains slowly mature
- **Late game:** everything converges — racing to finish landmark commissions, balancing all three factions, pushing Inspiration high enough to unlock the flourishing

Every earlier decision (which commissions to accept, which faction to favor, which apprentice to elevate) quietly builds toward this moment, giving those choices weight without making the game a race.

### Soft Ending

Reaching the Renaissance is a **soft ending** — a moment of celebration and arrival, not a screen that ends the game.

When all conditions are met:
- A **festival event** fires; named Masterworks light up across the skyline; a cinematic pan across everything the player built
- A title card: *"The Renaissance has come to your city."*
- Play continues, and the game shifts tone slightly into a flourishing to live in rather than build toward

**Post-Renaissance unlocks (Golden Age):**
- **Legacy system** — the player's Masterworks begin influencing other city-states (flavor + prestige bonuses)
- **Second-generation artists** — apprentices who trained under the original Masters become Masters themselves, creating a sense of cultural lineage
- **Harder commissions** — patrons from rival city-states arrive seeking the player's best artists, creating fresh tension
- **Golden Age baseline** — the city hums at a higher steady state

The feeling to aim for: *you made it, and now you get to live in what you built.*

### Modes

- **Campaign mode:** scenarios with specific goals that teach mechanics, building toward a first Renaissance
- **Sandbox mode:** open-ended play toward the Renaissance threshold, then continued Golden Age play

### Graphics track (parallel to the systems phases)

- **G2 — Terrain** *(done)*: single flat-shaded ground mesh — flat plain under the grid, rolling hills beyond, per-face grass tones + farmland patches as vertex colors; instanced cypress/olive tree scatter on the hills; fog-as-sky horizon.

- **G4 — Life & polish** *(done)*: chimney-smoke particles on active production buildings (stops when inactive — the activity-animation requirement); DOM landmark label pins projected over hub buildings; DefaultRenderingPipeline grade (contrast + warm vignette).
- **G5 — Stretch** *(open)*: river + bridge on one map edge, decorative citizens idling near the piazza (cosmetic only — must not grow simulation meaning), boats, animated banners, obelisk as the Memorial Column building.

## Key Design Principles (do not violate these)

1. **No citizen pathfinding micromanagement.** Roads are automatic. Citizens are abstracted.
2. **No granular supply chains.** Service buildings (bakery, tavern) simply unlock population thresholds — no food routing, no goods delivery simulation.
3. **Neighborhood zoning, not individual house placement.** Players zone areas; buildings fill in.
4. **Meaningful scarcity over complexity.** Material supplier capacity limits are the primary constraint — simple but impactful.
5. **Building diversity emerges from system design** (diminishing returns, complementary bonuses) — not arbitrary rules.
6. **Light spatial meaning.** Proximity to the Piazza matters. Cluster neighborhoods matter. But players are never punished for building something that looks good to them.
7. **Cozy but with real decisions.** Tension comes from commission deadlines, faction rivalry, and artist scarcity — not from crisis management or survival pressure.
8. **Lean resources.** Exactly three headline resources (Florins, Inspiration, Prestige) plus Population as a status pool. Each does a distinct job; only Inspiration needs active attention. Never add a resource the player doesn't make decisions about. Materials are capacity status, not a stockpile. Faction relationships replace any "patronage" currency.
9. **A building must unlock or boost — never require management.** If a building would need active operation, redesign or cut it.
10. **The goal is to achieve the Renaissance**, a confluence of conditions, ending softly into a Golden Age the player continues to inhabit.
